01 · Strategic Context

Why competitive pricing guardrails?

Amazon Merch had a one‑size‑fits‑all pricing approach that led to egregious prices, inconsistent pricing across similar designs, and a poor customer price perception. Benchmarking against SIC and 3P generics revealed that branded designs were often priced 5‑15% above comparable retailers (Target, etc.), while longtail designs had no logical price ceiling. The goal: align guardrails with customer obsession, ensure logical pricing across categories, and unlock incremental units without sacrificing profitability.

Key insight

For each –1% reduction in maximum price, we observed an average units uplift between 5.8% and 6.2% for hoodies, and 2.3% for t‑shirts in elastic markets (DE). These elasticities formed the basis of our 2025 pricing roadmap.

✔ Customer trust
Eliminate illogical pricing (e.g., generic design priced higher than branded). Build long‑term price perception.
✔ Designer trust
Avoid big‑bang changes; communicate experiments transparently. Pilot before full rollout.

04 · Price elasticity & data science methodology

How we measured the true impact of pricing changes

Because the pricing guardrail adjustment was not a randomised experiment, we had to use a quasi‑experimental design. This section walks you through the entire analytical process – from defining treatment/control groups, dealing with seasonality, to applying Propensity Score Matching (PSM) and Difference‑in‑Differences (DiD). The goal: isolate the causal effect of price reductions while accounting for confounding factors like holiday spikes, media events, and product lifecycles.

📅 Chronology of the analysis (FY24‑FY25)
Dec 2023
Pricing guardrails implemented – Fleece products (US) and longtail t‑shirts/hoodies (EU). Prices of ASINs above new maximum thresholds were automatically reduced.
Jan – Mar 2024
Data collection & cleaning – Extracted daily metrics: Glance Views (GV), Units Sold, OPS, Conversion Rate. 1% winsorization applied to limit outlier influence.
Apr – Jun 2024
Exploratory & YOY analysis – Compared before‑after and year‑over‑year. Found strong confounding: 68% of impacted longtail ASINs had zero sales in the prior year, making YOY useless.
Jul – Sep 2024
Quasi‑experimental design – Chose Difference‑in‑Differences (DiD) with Propensity Score Matching (PSM) to build a valid synthetic control group.
Oct – Dec 2024
Model execution & validation – Ran placebo tests to verify parallel trends assumption, then computed interaction coefficients for OPS, Units, GV, and Page Conversion.
Jan 2025 (ongoing)
Results & recommendation – Mixed statistical significance; directional positive impact. Recommended controlled experiment (pre‑defined treatment/control) for t‑shirts.
🎯 Step 1 – Define treatment
All fleece ASINs (US) and longtail hoodies/t‑shirts (EU) whose prices were reduced to the new guardrail after Dec 10, 2023. Average price decrease: –7.45% (longtail hoodies –9.04%).
⚖️ Step 2 – Build control universe
Fleece ASINs that had no price change after Dec 10, 2023, then filtered outliers (performance beyond treatment max). Initial pool: 26k longtail, 3.1k branded.
🔗 Step 3 – Propensity Score Matching (PSM)
Logistic regression to predict probability of being treated, using pre‑period metrics (GV, units, conversion, product category). Then KNN (k=10) to match control ASINs to treatment. Final control: ~6.8k ASINs.
📈 Step 4 – DiD regression
Model: Y = β₀ + β₁·treatment + β₂·after + β₃·(treatment×after) + ε. The interaction coefficient β₃ is the causal impact of the price change. We ran separate models for each product type and segment (branded/longtail).
📐 Why parallel trends matters

DiD assumes that treatment and control groups would have followed the same trend in the absence of the price change. We tested this with a placebo test (using a fake treatment date before the actual change). For branded hoodies, the placebo interaction p‑value was >0.05 → parallel trends held. For longtail hoodies, some deviations were corrected by PSM. Without PSM, the placebo would have shown pre‑existing differences, so PSM was essential.

📖 Glossary of terms (for non‑technical readers)

  • ▪ GV (Glance View) – Number of times a product detail page was viewed.
  • ▪ OPS (Ordered Product Sales) – Total sales revenue from the product.
  • ▪ PSM (Propensity Score Matching) – Statistical method to create a comparable control group by matching each treated ASIN with a similar untreated ASIN.
  • ▪ DiD (Difference‑in‑Differences) – Compares the change in outcome over time between treatment and control groups; the interaction term isolates the treatment effect.
  • ▪ Placebo test – A fake treatment date is used to check if groups were already diverging; if they were, the DiD results are invalid.
  • ▪ Winsorization – Replaces extreme outlier values with the 1st or 99th percentile to reduce noise.

⚠️ Limitations & takeaways

The fleece analysis could not achieve statistical significance due to limited sample size, overlapping confounding events (holidays, media), and the fact that only longtail prices changed in EU while branded remained unchanged. Therefore, we recommend a controlled experiment for t‑shirts: pre‑select a random set of ASINs for price reduction, keep another random set as control, and measure impact over a clean period without seasonality. This two‑way door test will provide definitive elasticity curves.
